Article One

What is a Chevrah Kadisha?

חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא

This is the first in a series of columns to teach about חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha and Jewish funeral practices.

Traditionally, the preparation of the deceased for burial has been taken care of by members of our Jewish community, called a חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha, which means "Holy Society." In Yiddish they are called לאַנדסמאַנשאַפֿטן Landsmanshaftn.

Why do we do this? It is because we desire to honor the deceased — מֵת meit (man) or מֵתָה meitah (woman) — with respect and dignity, and to ease the grief of the bereaved family and friends.

Jewish custom includes a specific traditional ritual to prepare a body for burial. This ritual is called טָהֳרָה Taharah and is performed by members of the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha. Volunteering to do טָהֳרָה Taharah is a very special מִצְוָה mitzvah, and the ritual is performed for all Jews — observant or not, affiliated or not.

When there is a death, the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha is notified, and we quickly assemble a team. This is all done anonymously and with respect. We are midwifing the deceased as the soul moves from this world to the next.

Article Two

Taharah — The Purification Ritual

טָהֳרָה

One of the main roles of the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha is to perform the ancient ritual of טָהֳרָה Taharah. טָהֳרָה Taharah means "pure" or "purify."

We have other rituals in our lives to purify ourselves — for example, נְטִילַת יָדַיִם Netilat Yadayim, the ritual washing of hands before meals, and immersing in a מִקְוֶה mikvah. The טָהֳרָה Taharah ritual is a spiritual washing — the final מִקְוֶה mikvah.

The חֶבְרָה Chevrah assembles a team of four to five members as quickly as possible after a person dies. This is done anonymously out of respect. A male team is provided for a man, and a female team for a woman. The חֶבְרָה Chevrah say prayers, then carefully and respectfully cleanse the מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah.

Next, the actual טָהֳרָה Taharah is performed by slowly pouring waters over the מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah from head to toe. This is מַיִם חַיִּים Mayim Chaim, the living waters. Then the מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah is dressed in the traditional תַּכְרִיכִים tachrichim — the burial shrouds — and then placed in the casket.

Our חֶבְרָה Chevrah is a volunteer group, and we will prepare any Jewish person for burial. Please contact us any time as you or a loved one is preparing for your final days, to learn more about this service and to express your wishes.

Article Three

Shmirah — Guarding the Deceased

שְׁמִירָה

שְׁמִירָה Shmirah, meaning "guarding," is a sacred Jewish practice in which we sit with a deceased person from the completion of טָהֳרָה Taharah until burial, ensuring they are never alone. This ancient tradition demonstrates profound respect for the deceased and offers comfort to the soul during its transition.

The practice is typically performed in two-hour shifts by volunteers known as שׁוֹמְרִים shomrim (guardians). During these shifts, guardians often recite Psalms, providing both a physical presence and spiritual comfort to the deceased. After the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha completes the טָהֳרָה Taharah, members continue the vigil until the funeral. This service is performed voluntarily and anonymously, though families may also invite close friends or family members to participate as שׁוֹמְרִים shomrim.

Why Shmirah Matters

  • Honor and Dignity: שְׁמִירָה Shmirah is considered one of the highest מִצְווֹת mitzvot because it is a pure act of kindness — the deceased cannot reciprocate, making it a truly selfless deed.
  • Comfort for the Bereaved: Knowing their loved one is being cared for and not left alone brings profound comfort to grieving families.
  • Spiritual Guidance: The presence of the שׁוֹמֵר shomer and the recitation of sacred texts help guide the soul on its journey from this world to the next.

Article Four

Tachrichim — Burial Garments

תַּכְרִיכִים

Our חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha follows the Jewish tradition of burying the deceased in simple white linen garments called תַּכְרִיכִים tachrichim. They are made from cotton, muslin, or linen with no zippers, fasteners, buttons, or metal.

תַּכְרִיכִים Tachrichim are created to parallel portions of what the High Priest wore during the time of the Temple. Yet the simplicity is to remind us to be humble. תַּכְרִיכִים Tachrichim do not have pockets, to remind us that we carry nothing material with us when we leave the physical world.

The outfit is similar for men and women, and consists of closed-foot pants with a tie at the waist, a shirt with a tie at the neck, a jacket with a collar and a tie at the neck, sometimes an apron for a woman, a face cover and bonnet for a woman, and a head cover for men. There are ties around the legs and a belt around the waist. Some people choose to be wrapped in their favorite טַלִּית tallit. Finally, a large sheet is wrapped around the מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah, like swaddling a baby, before they are placed in the casket.

The מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah is dressed by the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha after they have performed the טָהֳרָה taharah ritual. Dressing the מֵת / מֵתָה meit / meitah is called הַלְבָּשָׁה halbasha (dressing). When we dress our dead, we tie special knots representing the names of אֲדֹנָי Adonai. Everything we do during this ritual is to honor the dignity and holiness of the deceased.

Before dressing, we say a prayer called שׂוֹשׂ אָשִׂישׂ Sos Assis, with passages from Isaiah and Zechariah.

While most חֶבְרוֹת קַדִּישָׁא Chevrot Kadisha and funeral homes purchase תַּכְרִיכִים tachrichim from Rose Solomon, a commercial supplier in Brooklyn, there is a growing trend for חֶבְרוֹת קַדִּישָׁא Chevrot Kadisha to revive the practice of sewing תַּכְרִיכִים tachrichim locally. Article Five  ·  April 2026

Chevrah Kadisha Liturgy

לִיטוּרְגִיָּה

The חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha conducts two main functions: the טָהֳרָה Taharah — the preparation of the deceased for burial — and the שְׁמִירָה Shmirah — the watching and guarding of the deceased until the funeral. Both functions have a rich tradition of prayers in both Hebrew and English, composed of Psalms, passages from Song of Songs, and sections from prophets like Isaiah. The prayers have value for both the people doing the holy work and for the soul of the deceased.

The טָהֳרָה Taharah begins when the team assembles at the funeral home, usually within two days of death. Many urgent phone calls and texts have been made, and the team has had to adjust their daily activities to honor the person they will be spending a few intimate hours with. The first prayers are said in the lobby as we become grounded and prepare ourselves for holy work.

When we enter the room, we see the deceased on the table, covered with a sheet. We begin with a prayer asking the מֵת / מֵתָה Meit / Meitah to forgive us if we make any mistakes, and to let the נְשָׁמָה Neshamah (the soul, which is grammatically feminine in Hebrew) know that we will conduct ourselves with dignity, modesty, honor, and respect.

We begin by washing our hands for cleanliness and ritual purity, then recite the Prayer for Compassion — חָמוֹל Chamol — where we speak the name of the deceased and say: "May their Soul and Spirit rest with the Righteous."

As we wash the מֵת / מֵתָה Meit / Meitah, we work silently as one of the team reads or chants the prayers, readings from Song of Songs and Psalms. We chant words of comfort and confidence to the נְשָׁמָה Neshamah, who is now expanding beyond the confines of her incarnation.

After washing, we prepare for the ritual of living waters — the טָהֳרָה Taharah. As we take turns pouring 24 quarts of water from head to toe, we each say or chant three times:

טְהוֹרָה הִיא  ·  טָהוֹר הוּא  ·  טְהוֹרִים הֵם

טְהוֹרָה הִיא Tahoráh Hi  ·  טָהוֹר הוּא Tahor Hu  ·  טְהוֹרִים הֵם Tehorim Hem

"She is Pure!  ·  He is Pure!  ·  They are Pure!"

After drying, we dress the מֵת / מֵתָה Meit / Meitah in תַּכְרִיכִים Tachrichim and then place them gently in the casket. As we dress the deceased, we say the שׂוֹשׂ אָשִׂישׂ Sos Assis prayer. Before placing the deceased in the casket, we send the נְשָׁמָה Neshamah on her journey with the Priestly Blessing.

We end by saying the name of the deceased and asking for forgiveness for any act or omission that did not show respect, or that in any way might have caused offense.

During the שְׁמִירָה Shmirah, members of the חֶבְרָה קַדִּישָׁא Chevrah Kadisha sit with the deceased and pray, reading Psalms or poetry. There is no formal liturgy for שְׁמִירָה Shmirah. While the practice does not have the formal structure of the טָהֳרָה Taharah, it serves the purpose of being with the deceased in a quiet and meditative liminal space.

Article Six

Honoring the Dead Across Traditions

Jewish & Islamic Funeral Practices

Both Jewish and Islamic traditions place the highest importance on caring for the deceased with dignity, speed, and spiritual intention. Comparing our traditions reveals deep parallels — and meaningful differences — in how we accompany the dead on their final journey.

Islamic Funeral Practices

According to Islamic law (shariah), the body should be buried as soon as possible after death — a principle shared with Jewish tradition. A local Islamic community organization should be contacted immediately to help make arrangements, identify an appropriate funeral home, and coordinate with the family.

To prepare the body for burial, it must be washed (Ghusl) and shrouded (Kafan). Close same-sex family members are encouraged to perform the washing, though a spouse may also do so. The body is washed three times. This parallels the Jewish טָהֳרָה Taharah in its emphasis on ritual cleansing and same-gender care.

To shroud the body, three large white sheets of inexpensive material are laid on top of each other — a simplicity that mirrors the Jewish תַּכְרִיכִים tachrichim, reminding both traditions that all are equal in death.

Organ donation is generally acceptable in Islam, following the Qur'an's teaching: "Whosoever saves the life of one person it would be as if he saved the life of all mankind." Cremation, however, is forbidden (haram) in Islam.

After the funeral and burial, the immediate family gathers and receives visitors. It is customary for the community to provide food for the first few days of mourning — typically three days. This parallels the Jewish tradition of שִׁבְעָה shiva, during which the community provides meals and comfort to mourners.

What We Share

  • Prompt burial: Both traditions call for burial ideally within a day or two of death.
  • Simple white shrouds: Both use plain, inexpensive white garments, affirming equality and humility in death.
  • Ritual washing: Both involve careful, modest washing of the body by same-gender community members.
  • Community comfort: Both traditions gather to feed and comfort the bereaved in the days after burial.
  • Simplicity over display: Both discourage elaborate display in favor of dignity.
